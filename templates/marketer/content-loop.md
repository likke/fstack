# The Content Loop — How to Use AI Without Losing Your Brand Voice
*Learn this in 10 minutes. Use it for every piece of content you produce.*

---

## The Loop

```
/office-hours          → Validate the idea before you brief
       ↓
/brief-review MODE 1   → Review the brief before production
       ↓
     PRODUCE           → AI writes the draft
       ↓
/brief-review MODE 2   → Review AI output before publish
       ↓
/gtm-review            → Pressure-test the campaign
       ↓
    PUBLISH
```

Most marketers skip everything except "PRODUCE." That's why their content is generic, off-brand, and doesn't convert.

The loop is the system. The loop is what makes you unfireable.

---

## Step-by-Step Walk-Through: A LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post

**Scenario:** You're a marketing manager at a content agency. You want to post a LinkedIn thought leadership piece about content operations. Let's walk through the entire loop.

---

### STEP 1 — Office Hours: Validate the idea

**What you paste into ChatGPT or Claude:**

```
I want to write a LinkedIn post about content operations for agencies. Before I start, 
challenge me on this idea. Ask me what specific moment made me think of this. 
Tell me if this is the right thing to create right now. Be honest and direct — 
don't just validate me.
```

**What you get back (example):**

AI: "What specific client conversation or delivery failure made you think of this? If you can't name one, this idea might be too generic to land. Also — every content agency already posts about content operations. What's the angle that only YOU can write from? What's something counterintuitive or uncomfortable that you've seen that most people won't say?"

**What this does:** Forces you to find your specific angle before wasting a brief.

**Your refined idea:** "Write about how most agencies think their content chaos is a people problem — but it's actually a governance problem. Use a specific example from a client we fixed."

---

### STEP 2 — Brief Review MODE 1: Build a real brief

**What you paste into ChatGPT or Claude:**

```
Review this brief before I give it to AI:

BRIEF: Write a LinkedIn post for marketing directors at Philippine agencies 
who are embarrassed by how chaotic their content delivery is. 
Goal: make them feel understood, then curious about a better system. 
Tone: direct, dry, no fluff. 
Never say: leverage, unlock, seamless, scale your impact. 
Max 120 words. 
One CTA at the end: link to a free brand scan.
Success criteria: first line must make a marketing director stop scrolling.

Tell me if this brief is strong enough to produce a good output. 
Score it and rewrite anything that's vague.
```

**What you get back (example):**

AI: "Brief Score: Strong. Audience is specific, goal is clear, constraints are defined. One gap: 'make them feel understood then curious' is still vague. Add a concrete example of the chaos they're feeling — this will unlock much better copy. Suggested addition: 'Reference the specific pain: campaign deadline approaching, designer still hasn't seen the final copy, approval is in an email thread from last Tuesday.'"

**What this does:** Catches gaps in your brief before production — not after.

---

### STEP 3 — Produce: Give the improved brief to AI

**What you paste into ChatGPT or Claude:**

```
Write a LinkedIn post using this brief:

Audience: Marketing directors at Philippine agencies who are embarrassed by 
how chaotic their content delivery is. Specifically: campaign deadline approaching, 
designer still hasn't seen the final copy, approval sitting in an email thread 
from last Tuesday.
Goal: make them feel understood, then curious about a better system.
Tone: direct, dry, no fluff.
Never say: leverage, unlock, seamless, scale your impact.
Max 120 words.
CTA at the end: "Free brand scan → [link]"
Success criteria: first line must make a marketing director stop scrolling.
```

**What AI produces (example):**

"Your campaign is due Friday.

Your designer is still waiting for the final copy. The approval is in an email thread from last Tuesday. The brief was in Google Docs but someone moved it.

Nobody's fault. The system has no rules.

Here's what a governed content operation looks like instead: one place for briefs, automatic revision limits, client visibility without chasing anyone.

We ran this for a hotel chain last month. 30 assets. Zero revision rounds. 100% on time.

This is what we scan for free: [link]"

---

### STEP 4 — Brief Review MODE 2: Catch the problems before publishing

**What you paste into ChatGPT or Claude:**

```
Review this LinkedIn post before I publish it. Run these 5 checks:
1. Would a marketing director stop scrolling at the first line?
2. Does it sound like a real person wrote it, or does it sound like AI?
3. Are there specific numbers or examples — not just vague claims?
4. Is there one clear CTA?
5. Would my best client be proud to publish this?

Flag any problems and tell me what to fix. Then give me a verdict: Ship / Rewrite / Brief again.

POST:
[Paste the AI output here]
```

**What you get back (example):**

AI: "Scroll test: PASS — 'Your campaign is due Friday.' is specific and stops the scroll. Brand voice: PASS — no AI tells, short sentences, feels human. Specificity: PASS — '30 assets, zero revision rounds, 100% on time' is concrete proof. CTA: PASS — one clear action at the end. Best client test: PASS — this would resonate with any marketing director who's missed a deadline.

Verdict: Ship it. ✅

One optional improvement: if you have a real brand name you can use (with permission), replace 'a hotel chain' with the actual name — specific > generic every time."

---

### STEP 5 — GTM Review: Pressure-test before you boost or send

**What you paste into ChatGPT or Claude:**

```
I'm about to publish this LinkedIn post and potentially boost it as an ad. 
Pressure-test the campaign before I do:
1. Is this reaching the right person with the right pain?
2. Am I selling the outcome or the feature?
3. Will This Make Money? Grade this: A (closes a client in 30 days), 
   B (keeps a client), C (brand awareness), D (nice-to-have, skip it).
4. What's the kill criterion — when do I stop boosting this?

POST: [Paste the post here]
TARGET: Marketing Directors at Philippine agencies, 50-200 employees
```

**What you get back (example):**

AI: "Right person + right pain: PASS — specific enough to resonate. Outcome vs feature: PASS — leads with the chaos (pain) and proof (outcome), no feature language. Will This Make Money?: B — this is a nurture play. The CTA (free scan) builds toward a close in 60-90 days, not immediately. That's appropriate for LinkedIn organic.

Kill criterion: If the post gets fewer than 500 impressions from your target audience in 7 days → the targeting is wrong. If it gets good impressions but zero CTA clicks → rewrite the CTA.

Recommendation: Publish organic first. If engagement is strong (5+ comments, 2+ shares), boost to Marketing Directors in PH with ₱500 for 5 days. Review results after 5 days."

---

## PUBLISH ✅

You now have:
- A validated idea (not just a vague thought)
- A brief that produces good output
- AI copy that sounds like you
- A quality-checked post
- A campaign with a kill criterion

That's the loop. That's the system. That's what makes you unfireable.

## Quick Reference: The Loop in 5 Prompts

**Prompt 1 — Validate:** "Challenge this content idea. Ask me what specific moment made me think of this."

**Prompt 2 — Review brief:** "Score this brief. Tell me what's vague and rewrite anything that's missing."

**Prompt 3 — Produce:** "[Paste your finalized brief] Write the content."

**Prompt 4 — Review output:** "Review this content. Check: scroll test, brand voice, specificity, CTA, best client test. Verdict: Ship / Rewrite / Brief again."

**Prompt 5 — GTM check:** "Pressure-test this campaign. Right person + right pain? Outcome or feature? Will this make money (A/B/C/D)? What's the kill criterion?"
